Session 1 · Value · Key Takeaways
💎 Value: Key Takeaways
0 / 5 checked
✓
ROI starts before the project does.

If you don't define success metrics before launch, you're measuring noise after the fact. Baseline first, deploy second — no exceptions.

✓
Vanity metrics are the enemy of real value.

Model accuracy and uptime impress engineers — not CFOs. Only metrics tied to cost reduction, revenue impact, or time saved at scale truly matter.

✓
90 days is a forcing function, not a rule.

If you can't articulate what value you expect and when, you're funding a science experiment — not a business initiative.

✓
Non-financial ROI is real ROI — if you quantify it.

Speed, quality, employee experience, and decision confidence are legitimate value drivers. Convert them to measurable proxies — don't dismiss them.

✓
Know when to kill.

Mature AI orgs have a kill process, not just a build process. If a project can't show ROI trajectory within a defined window, cutting it IS the value decision.

Beyond the Pilot:
Arming Your Business with Agents
🤖 Gen AI — Doing Its Best
🦾 Agentic AI — Just Getting Started
Gen AI is powerful within a single prompt-response cycle. Agentic AI goes further — it plans, uses tools, observes results, and loops autonomously across multi-step workflows. The shift from Gen AI to Agents is the shift from assistant to employee.
🤖 Gen AI
Single prompt → single response
No memory between turns
No tool access
Human drives every step
🦾 Agentic AI
Plans & executes multi-step tasks
Persistent memory & context
Uses tools: search, email, APIs
Autonomous observe-act loop

Deploy — Panel Notes ✏️
NovelVista × GSDC Summit · Expert Panel Discussion
Session 3 of 5  ·  Before your post-assessment, here's what the panel said ↓
1
Business owns evaluation — not tech 🏢
The panel was clear: evaluation must be driven by business, not technology teams.
Tech builds it. Business decides if it's working. Guardrails must reflect business outcomes.
↓  follow this path...
2
The only safe deployment path 🛤️
POC → Pilot → Production
Pilot is controlled & limited — but in Prod, the number of ways to fail grows exponentially.
Scaling is a separate challenge. Don't skip stages.
↓
3
ROI checkpoints — not a final exam 📊
Don't wait until the end to measure ROI.
Evaluate at every stage — POC, Pilot, and Prod — so you can course-correct before it's too late.
↓  before you build...
4
Define the problem correctly first 🎯
Most deployments fail not because of bad AI — but because the wrong problem was solved.
Include the right stakeholders early. Problem definition is a group sport.
↓
5
Adoption comes after deployment — not before 🤝
The panel flagged Change Management as the most under-appreciated step.
Deployment → Adoption → Value is the real sequence.
Skipping adoption kills value even when the tech works perfectly.
↓  data reality check...
6
Don't wait for perfect data 🗂️
Data issues: duplication, incorrect values, historical gaps. They're normal — not blockers.
85% accurate data is good enough to start. A Validation Agent helps catch the rest.
Waiting for 100% means never shipping.
↓
7
Good code ≠ good product 🧪
Requirements and code can both be solid — but if testing is weak, you will find out in prod.
Many teams skip rigorous testing. That's where deployments break.
It's not about being "AI First" or "AI Native" — it's about solving the right problem.
AI as a label is noise. AI as a solution is signal. Lead with the problem, not the technology.
↓  the hidden risk...
8
Decay — interest fades over time ⏳
Especially for new AI products: excitement decays.
Stakeholder interest, team motivation, budget attention — all drift unless there's continuous visible value.
Build for sustained momentum, not just launch day.

☁️ Deploy: Key Takeaways
0 / 5 checked
✓
85% of pilots fail in production — and it's rarely the tech.

Change management, user adoption, and operational readiness kill more AI projects than bad models. Build the people plan before the model.

✓
Production needs a "definition of done" set before you build.

Experimentation and production require different mindsets. Without clear exit criteria, you're building forever.

✓
Hidden costs emerge AFTER deployment — budget for them.

Monitoring, retraining, support escalations, and drift correction often exceed the initial build cost within 12 months.

✓
An executive sponsor is non-negotiable.

Without one, your project gets de-prioritised the moment something urgent competes for budget or attention.

✓
Narrow use case first — then expand.

The fastest path to production is solving one problem at enterprise scale. Scope creep before production = guaranteed failure.

✏️ Panel Notes Orchestrate · Expert Discussion
Orchestrate — Panel Notes ✏️
NovelVista × GSDC Summit · Expert Panel Discussion
Session 4 of 5  ·  Before your post-assessment, here's what the panel said ↓
1
The workflow evolution 🔄
Multi-Human Workflow → Automate → Multi-Agent Workflow
Orchestration isn't just automation. It's the shift from humans coordinating tasks to agents coordinating tasks — with humans overseeing outcomes, not each step.
↓  understand the difference...
2
Agentic AI vs AI Agentic 🤖
Agentic AI — AI systems that have the power to make decisions and act autonomously. The AI is the agent.

AI Agentic — a broader approach where AI enables agentic behaviour across workflows, tools, and systems. The architecture is agentic, not just one model.
Knowing the difference matters when deciding how much autonomy to grant and where humans stay in the loop.
↓
3
Agents need context + least privilege 🔐
Two non-negotiables when orchestrating agents:
Context Transfer — each agent must know enough to act correctly without re-doing prior work.
Least Privilege — give agents only the access they need. No more. Limits blast radius when things go wrong.
↓  a common trap...
4
AI Journey Silos kill orchestration 🚧
When AI initiatives live in separate teams — marketing's AI, ops AI, finance AI — they can't talk to each other.
Silos prevent orchestration. Agents can only coordinate across systems that were designed to connect.
↓
5
Proof of Value — not Proof of Concept 🏆
The panel pushed back hard on POC culture.
A POC proves the tech can work. A Proof of Value proves it's worth running in production.
Orchestration projects must show real business value early — not just a working demo.
↓  the golden rule...
6
Keep it Simple and Safe 🛡️
The more agents you chain, the more failure points you create.
Start with the simplest orchestration that solves the problem.
Safety isn't optional — it's the foundation every agentic system must be built on.

Key Takeaways
🧩
Start with small, single-purpose agents — each owning one task, one tool, one decision. The orchestrator's job is coordination, not execution. Build the agents first; the orchestrator earns its complexity.

Customer experience is the most ruthless test of orchestration. If the handoff between agents feels broken — humans notice immediately.

1
Training alone isn't enough 📋
The panel opened with a clear warning: a training plan without a strategy is just a calendar of events.

The most effective learning is hands-on and applied — not passive consumption.
Training must be designed around real work: give people problems to solve with AI, not slides to watch.

Redesign roles — not just training 🔄
Training without role redesign is wasted.
If an associate learns an AI skill but their job doesn't change to encourage applying it, that skill disappears within weeks.
Roles must be redesigned so using AI becomes part of the job — not an extra.
↓  sustain it through...
4
Build a Community of Practitioners 🤝
Individual training fades. Communities sustain capability.
Form a Community of Practice where people share what's working, help each other, and keep each other accountable.
This is how learning becomes culture.

Completion rates → Assessment scores → Business Impact 💰
"X% of employees completed the course" is a vanity metric.

Your AI hiring tool shows 15% bias against women candidates. The tool improves hiring speed by 40%. Do you:

Shut it down immediately

🟢 C — Pragmatic balance. Human oversight mitigates harm while you fix the root cause. Most governance frameworks recommend this hybrid approach.
2
🏥
A client's AI chatbot gave incorrect medical advice. No harm occurred yet. Do you:
A
Disclose to all clients immediately
B
Fix silently and add safeguards
C
Disclose only to the affected client
🟢 A — Transparency builds trust. In regulated sectors (health/finance), proactive disclosure is often legally required. Short-term reputation hit, long-term credibility gain.
🔴 B — Silent fixes are a governance red flag. If discovered later, the cover-up causes far more damage than the original error. Avoid this path.
🟡 C — Partial disclosure may satisfy immediate obligations but misses the broader user base at risk. Check your legal obligations — partial disclosure may not be enough.

Your competitor launched an AI product without proper testing. You can beat them to market if you skip governance. Do you:
A
Launch fast, govern later

Stick to your governance process

Do minimal viable governance and launch

🟢 B — The right call. Your governance process exists for this moment. Clients who chose you for reliability will stay. The race to the bottom hurts everyone.
🟡 C — Reasonable compromise if "minimal viable governance" is genuinely meaningful and not just a label. Define what that threshold is clearly before proceeding.

Govern — Panel Notes ✏️
NovelVista × GSDC Summit · Expert Panel Discussion
Session 5 of 5  ·  Before your post-assessment, here's what the panel said ↓
1
The 5 pillars of AI Governance 🏛️
📦 Inventory 👤 Accountability 📜 Policy 👁️ Monitoring 📊 Reporting
Governance isn't one thing — it's these five working together.
Miss any one pillar and your governance framework has a hole.
↓  the hardest question...
2
Who is accountable for outcomes? 🎯
The panel had a live debate on this. Shared responsibility often means no responsibility.
The answer: define a Governance Council — a named group that maps who is accountable for what, across every AI system in use.
"Everyone is responsible" and "someone is responsible" are very different things.
↓  learn from regulation...
3
EU AI Act — 3 lessons for every org 🌍
Europe's AI regulation sets the benchmark. Three things every organisation should borrow:
1. Proper Documentation — every AI system must have a clear record of what it does, how it was trained, and what decisions it makes.

2. Data Governance — know where your data comes from, who owns it, and how private data is managed and protected.

3. Human in the Loop — for high-stakes decisions, define exactly which human is reviewing the AI's output and what action they take. Vague HITL is no HITL.
↓
4
Purpose Creep — the silent risk ⚠️
Purpose Creep happens when an AI system is quietly repurposed beyond its original intent.
Example: A chatbot built to answer HR FAQs is later used to screen job applicants. The original risk assessment, data scope, and approvals were never designed for hiring decisions — but now the system is making them.

Whenever the purpose changes, the risk must be re-evaluated from scratch.
↓  accept reality first...
5
Shadow AI is already here — accept it 👤
The panel agreed: Shadow AI is common and growing. Employees are already using AI tools that IT and leadership haven't approved.
Trying to ban it doesn't work. The right response is to accept it, surface it, and bring it into your governance framework — before it causes a data or compliance incident.
↓  the governance principle...
6
If it's observable — it must be trackable 🔭
The closing principle from the panel:
Anything your AI system does that can be observed should be tracked.
Decisions, outputs, errors, overrides, escalations — if it happens, it should leave a trace.
Governance without observability is just policy on paper.
